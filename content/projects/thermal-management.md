Heat is not a side effect in an EV. It's a primary variable.

The battery has a performance window — roughly 15–35°C for optimal operation. Below that, lithium diffusion slows, resistance climbs, and the cell can't deliver rated power. Above that, degradation accelerates and, at the extreme end, you're looking at thermal runaway. The thermal management system exists to keep everything inside that window, under all operating conditions, for the life of the vehicle.

That's the problem. It sounds manageable until you realize how many things are generating and moving heat simultaneously.

---

## What the System Actually Looks Like

The thermal model covers the full chain:

- **Battery pack** — heat generated by I²R losses and electrochemical reactions, distributed unevenly across cells depending on pack geometry and cell-to-cell variation
- **Power electronics** — inverter and DC-DC converter have their own heat sources and their own failure modes if overtemperature
- **Coolant loop** — the hydraulic network that connects all of these to the heat exchanger, with its own pressure drops, flow rates, and thermal lag
- **Cabin HVAC** — competes for cooling capacity with the battery, especially in summer at high load

The coupling is what makes this hard. If the cabin wants more cooling, it pulls from the same refrigerant loop the battery is using. If the inverter runs hot during a long uphill section, the coolant entering the battery is already warm. These aren't independent problems you can optimize separately.

---

## How the Model Was Built

Lumped thermal networks — each component is a node with thermal mass and conductance paths to its neighbors. Transient behavior is captured by ODE integration. Fast enough to run at vehicle-simulation timescales, physically grounded enough to capture the important dynamics.

The calibration was against instrumented vehicle test data across ambient conditions: cold soak start (-20°C), motorway cruise, stop-start city driving, DC fast charge. Each scenario exercises a different part of the model.

The telling moments are always at the **transitions** — the first few minutes of a cold start, the switchover from charge to drive, the end of a long highway section going into a city. Steady-state is easy. Transient response is where models reveal their weaknesses.

---

## The Coupling That Changes Everything

Battery thermal behavior isn't independent of electrochemistry. Temperature affects:

- Ion diffusion rate (Arrhenius — exponential sensitivity)
- Open circuit voltage (entropic heating term)
- Aging rate (calendar degradation doubles roughly every 10°C)
- Internal resistance (which determines heat generation in the first place)

So the thermal model and the electrochemical model are actually coupled — changes in one affect the other on timescales from milliseconds (Joule heating) to years (degradation). Most production models treat this loosely. The correct approach is tight two-way coupling, which adds complexity but is necessary for anything involving aging prediction or extreme-condition safety assessment.

---

## Patterns I Keep Seeing

**Thermal problems usually show up at boundaries.** The failure is rarely "cell too hot in the middle of the pack." It's the corner cell, the one with a slightly worse cooling path, that sees the highest temperature delta. The geometry creates the gradient. The gradient creates the failure.

**The system has memory.** A pack that was fully charged in a hot garage and then immediately driven fast is in a worse state than one pre-conditioned at 25°C. The thermal history matters for both performance and aging. Models that don't carry state across operating modes miss this.

---

## What This Work Feeds Into

- Charging strategy optimization: the fastest charge that doesn't push temperature above the aging threshold
- Pack design validation: whether the cooling layout is uniform enough to prevent cell-to-cell divergence
- End-of-life prediction: how thermal history accumulates into capacity fade over years
